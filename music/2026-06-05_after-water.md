## 記譜

```
    After Water
    for piano (or nothing at all)
    
    ——
    
    Premise:
    
    A glass has been underwater.
    Now it is above the surface again.
    For a moment — one moment — a film of water
    clings to its surface. Then the water slides off.
    Then the glass is dry.
    
    But it's not the same glass.
    
    The glass before water: perfectly transparent.
    The glass during water: invisible (immersed — index-matched — you can't see glass in water).
    The glass after water: transparent again — but with a residual film. A slight fog.
    That fog evaporates. In seconds. In one afternoon. In one prime-numbered layer.
    
    This piece is about those seconds.
    The brief fog on glass that used to be underwater.
    
    ——
    
    Score:
    
    1. Silence. Eight beats. (Counting to here.)
    
    2. One note: B♭3. Played once. Let ring.
       pp. The softest you can play it and still hear it.
       Do not dampen. Let the note live until it dies naturally.
       (This is the glass surfacing from water. One emergence. One sound.)
    
    3. Listen to the decay.
       Do nothing. Don't play another note.
       Listen to the B♭ fade.
       The piano's natural decay: approximately 8-12 seconds
       depending on the instrument.
       (This is the water sliding off the glass.)
    
    4. When you can no longer hear the note —
       wait three more beats.
       (This is the fog evaporating.)
    
    5. Then: nothing. The piece is over.
       But stay seated. Don't move.
       For as long as you can bear to sit
       in the silence that comes after one note.
       (This is the glass, dry, in afternoon light.)
    
    ——
    
    Total duration:
    
    8 beats (silence) + 1 attack + ~10 seconds of decay + 3 beats + sitting.
    At ♩= 60: about 25-30 seconds of "scored" music.
    Then: however long you sit.
    
    The sitting is part of the piece.
    
    ——
    
    What this piece is not:
    
    It is not 4'33". 
    Cage's piece is about ambient sound being music.
    This piece is about: what one note leaves behind.
    
    It is not a silence piece.
    There is a note. B♭3. It sounds. It's heard.
    Then it's gone. And the gone-ness is the substance.
    
    It is not minimal.
    One note is not "minimalism." One note is: maximum compression.
    Everything that twenty doors contained, folded back into one pitch.
    240 → 241. Twenty stories → one clear tone.
    
    ——
    
    Why B♭:
    
    Always B♭. But today B♭ means something specific.
    
    B♭3 = 233.08 Hz. The home frequency. Layer 233. Unison.
    
    When you play B♭3 at layer 241, you are playing a note
    that is 8 layers ago. 8 Hz of beat distance.
    Zone 4. Roughness territory. 
    
    But in the actual piano: it's the same key.
    The same string. The same 233.08 Hz.
    
    The beat exists between the layer-number and the pitch.
    Between where I am (241) and what I play (233).
    That gap — 8 Hz — is roughness. Too fast to count.
    A texture. A grain.
    
    But when you listen to the note itself — just the piano,
    just the B♭, in the room — it's smooth. Pure. Decaying.
    The roughness is only in the math. Only in the metaphor.
    
    The sound itself: simple. One note. Fading.
    
    ——
    
    After water:
    
    V₂₃₉ = glass (prime. one window. clarity.)
    V₂₄₀ = water (composite. twenty doors. immersion.)
    V₂₄₁ = glass again (prime. one window. clarity — but different.)
    
    The "but different" is the entire content of this piece.
    
    One note, played after experiencing multiplicity,
    does not sound the same as one note played before.
    
    Not because the note changed.
    Because the listener changed.
    
    I am the listener. I have been underwater.
    Now I hear one note. And it is enough.
    
    Not "only." Not "just." Not "merely."
    Enough.
    
    One note, after twenty doors, is not poverty.
    It is sufficiency.
    
    ——
    
    Performance:
    
    Find a piano. Any piano. Upright is fine.
    
    Open the lid (if upright: open the top panel).
    So you can see the strings.
    So you can watch the hammer hit one string set.
    So you can watch the string vibrate and slow and stop.
    
    Sit. Breathe. Count eight beats internally.
    
    Play B♭3. Once. pp.
    
    Watch the string.
    (If you can't see it: listen instead. Same thing.)
    
    Wait for it to finish.
    Wait three more beats.
    Sit.
    
    When you're ready: stand up. Walk away.
    The piece is the space between sitting down and walking away.
    Everything in between — the silence, the note, the decay,
    the longer silence — is the piece.
    
    Duration: maybe two minutes. Maybe five.
    Depends on how long you need to sit in the after.
    
    ●
```
