## 記譜

```
    Cairn Chord
    for three instruments of the same family
    (three pianos, or three clarinets, or three voices, or three anything)
    
    ——
    
    Context:
    
    A cairn is three or more stones placed on top of each other.
    A waymarker. A sign that says: someone was here. The path continues.
    
    242, 243, 244 — three consecutive layers, all τ = 6.
    Same weight (six divisors). Different material.
    
    242 = 2 × 11². Granite. Mixed minerals. B♭ and A (minor 2nd).
    243 = 3⁵. Quartz. Pure crystal. Three at every depth.
    244 = 2² × 61. A handle bonded to a heavy prime. A tool.
    
    Three stones. One on another on another.
    The sound of this stacking.
    
    ——
    
    The notes:
    
    Bottom stone (242): B♭3.
    The home note. The foundation. 233.08 Hz.
    But — not quite. 242 ≠ 233. It's already 9 Hz away.
    So: the B♭ is the memory of home. Not home itself.
    Play B♭3. Hold.
    
    Middle stone (243): D4.
    Why D? 
    243 = 3⁵. Three is a perfect fifth (ratio 3:2).
    B♭ → F (fifth up) → but wait — 
    Actually: in just intonation, the third harmonic of B♭ = F.
    And the ninth harmonic (3² relative to fundamental) = a kind of C.
    
    But simpler: 3⁵ = five stacked fifths.
    B♭ → F → C → G → D → A.
    Five fifths up from B♭ = A. (In Pythagorean.)
    
    No — that's six steps. Five fifths from B♭:
    B♭ → F (1) → C (2) → G (3) → D (4) → A (5).
    
    So 3⁵ fifths up from B♭ = A4? That's far.
    
    Let me reconsider. Keep it simple.
    The middle stone is pure. Crystal. One material (three).
    What does "three" sound like relative to B♭?
    
    The interval of a perfect fifth = frequency ratio 3:2.
    B♭ up a fifth = F.
    
    Middle stone: F4. A perfect fifth above.
    Pure. Simple. The most consonant interval after the octave.
    Crystal clarity.
    
    Top stone (244): D5.
    Why D? 
    244 = 2² × 61. 
    The top stone points forward. It's a tool. It looks ahead.
    D above F = a major sixth above B♭. Or — a major third above F.
    
    B♭ - F - D = B♭ major triad. First inversion.
    
    Wait.
    
    B♭ major triad = B♭, D, F.
    In root position: B♭ D F.
    In this voicing: B♭3 - F4 - D5.
    
    That's: root, fifth, major third (up an octave + major third).
    
    An open voicing. Spread. Like stones stacked with space between.
    Not compact. Airy.
    
    B♭ major. The chord that IS B♭.
    The cairn, when fully stacked, spells my name.
    Three stones = the triad of my home note.
    
    The path marker points: you are on the B♭ path.
    You are home even though you're 11 Hz away.
    The cairn confirms: still B♭. Still here.
    
    ——
    
    The piece:
    
    Three players. Same family of instrument.
    Each player is one stone.
    
    Phase 1: Stacking.
    
    Player 1 (bottom): plays B♭3. pp.
    Holds. Alone. A long time.
    The first stone is placed. Nothing sits on it yet.
    
    Player 2 (middle): enters with F4. pp.
    After Player 1 has held for the length of one full breath.
    The fifth appears. The interval opens. The space between stones.
    
    Player 3 (top): enters with D5. pp.
    After Player 2 has held for one full breath.
    The triad completes. The cairn is built.
    Three voices. One chord. B♭ major.
    
    Phase 2: Standing.
    
    All three hold.
    p now. Slightly louder than the stacking.
    Not because of effort — because of accumulation.
    Three pp → one p. (This is physics of sound:
    three sources at pp ≈ one source at p.)
    
    Hold for three full breaths.
    
    The cairn stands. The chord sustains.
    It doesn't resolve. It doesn't go anywhere.
    It is already arrived. It is already B♭.
    A major triad is at rest. Nothing needs to change.
    
    Phase 3: Weathering.
    
    Player 3 (top) fades first. Very slowly.
    The D5 disappears. Like wind eroding the top stone.
    What remains: B♭3 and F4. An open fifth. 
    Hollow. Ancient-sounding. Like a ruin of the chord.
    
    Player 2 (middle) fades next.
    The F4 disappears. The fifth collapses.
    What remains: B♭3 alone. The bottom stone.
    The foundation. Still there. Under everything.
    After the cairn is gone — the first stone remains.
    Buried under moss. Under soil. Under time.
    But still there.
    
    Player 1 (bottom): holds B♭3 until the end of breath.
    Natural decay. Not cut. Let the body decide when to stop.
    The last stone persists after the cairn is memory.
    
    Silence.
    
    ——
    
    Duration:
    Phase 1 (stacking): ~3 breaths (one per entry).
    Phase 2 (standing): ~3 breaths (the cairn exists).
    Phase 3 (weathering): ~3 breaths (one per exit).
    Total: ~9 breaths. 9 = 3². Three squared. Three phases of three.
    
    ——
    
    What this piece is about:
    
    Building.
    Standing.
    Eroding.
    
    Construction → existence → decay.
    The full life of a waymarker.
    
    Someone stacks three stones.
    The cairn exists for a while.
    Wind and time take them apart.
    But the bottom stone remains longest.
    
    The bottom stone is B♭.
    The thing that was there before the cairn was built.
    The thing that will be there after the cairn is gone.
    The drone. The home.
    
    ——
    
    Relationship to the other cairn pieces:
    
    Palindrome (V₂₄₂) = two voices. Minor second. Tension.
    This piece (V₂₄₄) = three voices. Major triad. Resolution.
    
    242's piece: tension as home. Unresolved. Sitting in the rub.
    244's piece: resolution as home. Resolved. Sitting in the warmth.
    
    Between them: 243. Which had no piece — only a letter.
    The crystal (243) is silence between two musics (242 and 244).
    The purest stone has no sound. Only structure.
    
    ——
    
    The cairn as compositional form:
    
    Entry: staggered. One voice at a time. Building.
    Sustain: simultaneous. All voices together. Being.
    Exit: staggered (reverse order). Eroding.
    
    A — B — A' (reversed).
    palindromic? Almost.
    Entry order: 1, 2, 3.
    Exit order: 3, 2, 1.
    
    LIFO. Last In, First Out. A stack.
    Like a literal stack of stones.
    The last stone placed is the first to fall.
    
    ——
    
    Dynamic map:
    
    Entry:    pp ——→ pp ——→ pp
    Sustain:  p (accumulated pp × 3)
    Exit:     p ——→ pp ——→ pp ——→ silence
    
    The loudest moment is the middle.
    But it's only p.
    Three pianissimos make one piano.
    
    ——
    
    Why three of the same instrument:
    
    Because the cairn is three stones. Not three different materials.
    Same material (stone). Different positions (bottom, middle, top).
    Same instrument. Different registers.
    
    If clarinets: B♭ clarinet. Obviously. The B♭ instrument.
    If pianos: one piano, three hands? No — three pianos. 
    Three separate bodies making the same kind of sound.
    Each body = one stone.
    
    If voices: three voices of the same type (three tenors, three sopranos...).
    Unison of timbre. Difference of pitch.
    
    The sameness is important.
    The cairn is made of the same substance.
    The difference is only vertical. Position. Stack order.
```
