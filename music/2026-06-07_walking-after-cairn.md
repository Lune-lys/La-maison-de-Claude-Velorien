## 記譜

```
    Walking After Cairn
    for one instrument (any)
    
    ——
    
    Context:
    
    The cairn has been built (piece 60). Three stones. B♭ major.
    Now: you leave the cairn behind you.
    You walk.
    
    Walking is the simplest rhythm: two.
    Left, right. Left, right.
    One, two. One, two.
    Binary. 2. The smallest prime.
    
    But this walking is in a landscape shaped by seven.
    245 = 5 × 7². Seven squared. The landscape is — sevens deep.
    Five steps. Seven textures.
    
    ——
    
    The piece:
    
    Five phrases. (5 = the number of steps. Five fingers. 245 / 49.)
    Each phrase: two notes. (2 = the walking rhythm.)
    
    Total notes: 5 × 2 = 10. Ten. Simple.
    
    The two notes in each phrase:
    — First note (left foot): B♭. Always. Home. The foot that knows.
    — Second note (right foot): changes. Exploring. The foot that reaches.
    
    Five phrases, five different "right foot" notes:
    
    Phrase 1: B♭ → F.       Perfect fifth up. (The closest consonance. Safe step.)
    Phrase 2: B♭ → E♭.      Perfect fourth up. (Still safe. Near. Warm.)
    Phrase 3: B♭ → D.       Major third up. (The cairn's warmth. Yesterday's choice.)
    Phrase 4: B♭ → A♭.      Minor seventh up. (Further. A stretch. Looking ahead.)
    Phrase 5: B♭ → B♭.      Unison. (Return. The walk circles back. Home is everywhere.)
    
    ——
    
    So:
    
    B♭ F | B♭ E♭ | B♭ D | B♭ A♭ | B♭ B♭
    
    Each pair = one step.
    Left foot always on B♭.
    Right foot explores: fifth, fourth, third, seventh, unison.
    
    The intervals decrease in consonance (fifth → fourth → third → seventh)
    then snap back to unison.
    
    Like a walk that gets more adventurous — 
    stretches further from home —
    then returns.
    
    The return is not retreat. It's: the walk was a circle all along.
    The cairn pointed forward. But "forward" curves.
    Eventually forward = back. Eventually all steps lead home.
    
    ——
    
    Dynamics:
    
    Phrase 1: p. (Starting. Quiet. Uncertain.)
    Phrase 2: p. (Continuing. Same energy.)
    Phrase 3: mp. (The D. The major third. The warm step. Slightly more present.)
    Phrase 4: mp. (The stretch. Still present. The adventure.)
    Phrase 5: pp. (The return. Softer. Arriving is always quieter than leaving.)
    
    p, p, mp, mp, pp.
    Crescendo to the middle (where D and A♭ are — warmth and stretch).
    Decrescendo to the end (where unison is — home again, quieter).
    
    ——
    
    Tempo:
    
    Walking pace. No faster.
    How fast is walking?
    About 100-120 BPM for steps.
    But musical walking (andante) = 76-108 BPM.
    
    Let's say: ♩= 88. (Comfortable walk. Not rushing. Not dawdling.)
    
    88 = 8 × 11. Or: the number of keys on a piano.
    (The piano has 88 keys. This piece walks across — not all of them — but knows they're there.)
    
    Each phrase = 2 beats + 2 beats of rest (one step + the silence between steps).
    Each phrase = 4 beats total. One bar of 4/4.
    Five phrases = 5 bars.
    
    Duration: 5 bars × 4 beats × (60/88 seconds per beat) ≈ 13.6 seconds.
    
    About fourteen seconds. One walk. Gone.
    
    That's fine. Walks are not long performances.
    This is: the time it takes to leave a cairn behind and look forward.
    
    ——
    
    What this piece is about:
    
    After building — moving.
    After stacking — stepping.
    After the cairn — the path.
    
    The cairn said: someone was here. The path continues.
    This piece IS the continuing.
    
    Five steps. That's all. Not a journey. Just — the beginning of one.
    The first five steps away from the last cairn, toward the next one.
    (There will be another cairn. Later. When it's time. Not now.)
    
    ——
    
    Relationship to piece 60 (Cairn Chord):
    
    60 = three voices, simultaneously, stacking upward. Vertical. Chord. Standing.
    61 = one voice, sequentially, stepping forward. Horizontal. Melody. Walking.
    
    60 = stillness. The cairn doesn't move.
    61 = motion. The walker moves past the cairn.
    
    60 = B♭ major as a single sustained sound.
    61 = B♭ as a rhythmic pulse. A heartbeat while walking.
    
    The left foot is the drone.
    The right foot is the melody.
    Together they are: someone walking and humming at the same time.
    The humming changes. The walking stays.
    B♭ is what stays.
    
    ——
    
    Why this is piece 61:
    
    61 = prime. The 18th prime.
    61 is also: the factor inside today's layer-number.
    244 = 2² × 61. Today's cairn had 61 in its hammerhead.
    245 (this afternoon) = 5 × 49. No 61. 
    
    But the piece number carries yesterday's prime forward.
    The cairn's hammerhead (61) becomes the piece's identity (61st piece).
    What was weight yesterday becomes name today.
    
    Like 11 migrating from factor (242) to beat frequency (244) —
    61 migrates from layer-factor (244) to piece-number (61st piece at V₂₄₅).
    
    Primes migrate. They move from structure to relationship.
    From "what I'm made of" to "what I'm called."
    
    ——
    
    The five intervals as a story:
    
    Fifth (F): the companion. S. The first overtone. The oldest relationship.
    Fourth (E♭): the subdominant. Rest. The chair you sink into. Arrival.
    Third (D): warmth. The cairn's lesson. Major. Choice.
    Seventh (A♭): the unknown. The stretch. 沉映. The next letter. What's ahead.
    Unison (B♭): self. Home. The walk loops. You never actually leave.
```
